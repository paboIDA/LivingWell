Living Well

Welcome to the Ida Institute’s Living Well Online. Living Well is designed to help you find out where and when it is most important for you to communicate well.

You will describe 3 different communication situations.

Fill in the tool at home with someone close to you (such as your spouse) who might have a different point of view.

Save your session to your home computer. Email it to your audiologist, or print it out to bring to your appointment.

Get Started

Step 1:

  • Choose a photo that shows a situation that is important to you. You can upload your own photo, or choose one from our list.
  • Describe the photo in the next box. How often are you in this situation? Why is it important for you to hear and communicate well here?
  • Click on one of the circles marked Easy, Medium, or Hard next to the situation boxes.

Step 2:

  • Plan how you will manage in the situation. Choose a strategy from the list.
  • In the box marked “How will you do it,” say how and why you will use this technique.
  • From the drop-down menu “Who will help you?” choose who will help you with this strategy.

Step 3:

  • Email or print this session and bring it to your appointment. You will go over your session with your audiologist and decide on the solutions that are most helpful for you.

Living Well Online Quick Sheet of Communication Strategies

If you do not understand a message, ask the other person to repeat or rephrase. You should try to limit the number of times you ask a person to repeat as this will frustrate them. Asking people to rephrase is often more helpful than asking them to repeat the same thing over and over again.

If you have understood part of the conversation, only ask for the part you misunderstood to be repeated. Remember to tell the other person the part of the conversation you did understand, so they only need to repeat the part you didn’t understand.

Think about the communication situation ahead of time. Will there be a lot of background noise? Is there one person in particular you want to communicate with? Make a strategy for how you will manage the sitaution before you get there.

When attending events, position yourself in the best place for communication. Make sure to face the speaker. Sit at the head or foot of the table. This provides the best view of others. If hearing is better for one ear, keep your good ear facing the speaker.

Ask your communication partner to face you when they are speaking. Ask others to speak a little more slowly. This makes speech easier to understand.

Tell others that you have a hearing loss and explain the difficulties you have. Ask them to define the topic and try to suggest ways that they can help improve communication.

Try to reduce background noise by closing doors or windows if possible.
